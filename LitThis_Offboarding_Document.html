02 / Four What we built together

The work, in summary.

Beyond the headline numbers, the engagement leaves behind a restructured account, a disciplined operating rhythm, and a set of optimizations that continue to compound. This is what changed under the hood.

i. Sales trajectory across the engagement

ii. Two deliberate phases of efficiency

Q4 2025 — Aggressive scale

We deliberately leaned into peak season, widening targeting and increasing bids on high-converting placements. ACOS rose temporarily to 45% in November — a conscious trade-off to win Q4 volume. The result was the $33,623 December peak, the largest month in the account's history.

Q1–Q2 2026 — Efficiency pivot

With Q4 over and seasonal demand softening, we pulled spend back to protect margin rather than force volume against a cooler market. April 2026 is now running at 22.95% ACOS and 16.47% TACOS — the strongest combined efficiency of the entire engagement.

iii. Key optimizations delivered

  • Full Search Term Report audit & wasted-spend elimination. Reset the efficiency baseline at takeover by negativizing non-converting spend and freeing budget for proven winners.
  • Full account restructure. Top performers isolated into dedicated campaigns with granular bid control — the structural foundation that made the Q4 scale-up possible without losing efficiency on core terms.
  • Price increase absorbed cleanly. When pricing moved from $19.99 to $21.99 in January 2026, bid strategy was adjusted to protect conversion. Q1 2026 sales landed 160% above Q1 2025 at the higher price.
  • Creator Connections activated. A new traffic source outside the Amazon search auction, already producing attributed orders from a small initial footprint.
  • Dayparting data set built. Three months of Scale Insights data identified consistent day-of-week patterns (Wed/Thu strong, Tue/Sat weak) — a data layer that's now in place and ready to use.

iv. The organic flywheel

Across the engagement, organic share of units climbed from roughly 48% at takeover to 73% in April 2026. This is the signal that matters most for the long term — it means the paid work was lifting organic rank, not cannibalizing it. A listing that earns 70%+ of its units organically is a structurally healthier asset than one dependent on paid media for the majority of its sales.

03 / Four A candid note on what comes next

Pausing PPC — what to expect.

Reallocating the PPC budget to other channels is a legitimate decision and one we fully support. This section is not a counter-pitch. It is a plain reading of how Amazon listings typically behave when paid traffic stops, so the team can plan realistically rather than be surprised later.

i. What the data on this account specifically shows

Roughly 27% of LitThis's current units come through PPC; the remaining 73% are organic. That organic share, however, was earned in part by the keyword impressions, click-through, and conversion velocity that paid traffic has been generating consistently for eight months. Amazon's ranking algorithm rewards recent sales velocity on a keyword — when paid clicks stop on those keywords, the velocity signal weakens, and rank tends to drift downward over the following weeks.

ii. The pattern, simply stated

  • Weeks 1–4 after pause. Sales typically hold close to current levels — organic rank carries the listing on its existing momentum.
  • Weeks 4–12. As competitors continue bidding on shared keywords, organic rank on those terms gradually softens. Most listings see a 20–40% decline in monthly sales over this window, depending on category competitiveness.
  • Beyond 12 weeks. The listing settles into a new equilibrium that reflects pure organic strength and review authority — stable, but typically lower than the paid-supported peak.

These ranges are directional, drawn from how comparable accounts have behaved when paid traffic was paused. The actual trajectory for LitThis will depend on competitor aggressiveness, seasonality, and whether external traffic (creators, off-Amazon channels) is layered in to partially offset the paid signal.

iii. A note on restarting later

If PPC is restarted at a future date — whether by AMZ ONESTEP or another partner — it is worth knowing that regaining momentum usually takes longer than the original ramp-up. The first time around, the listing built rank from a standing start in roughly 90 days. After a pause, the same campaigns typically take 60–90 days just to re-establish keyword relevance and conversion history that Amazon's algorithm had previously cached. This is not unique to this account; it is a function of how the platform weighs recency.

Practical takeaway

The eight months of work has built durable foundations — restructured campaigns, negative keyword libraries, dayparting data, an organic rank base. These do not disappear when spend pauses. But the velocity signal does, and that's the part worth planning around — both for the next 90 days, and if PPC is ever switched back on.

iv. What we recommend monitoring

  • Organic rank on the top 10 keywords — track weekly for the first 8 weeks. Movement on these terms is the leading indicator of where total sales will land.
  • Session and unit-session percentage in Brand Analytics. If sessions hold but conversion drops, it's a listing issue; if sessions drop, it's a rank issue.
  • Review velocity. A steady trickle of organic reviews matters more in the absence of paid traffic — it's now the primary engine for conversion-rate stability.
  • Competitor pricing and new entrants on the top 5 head terms, especially through Q4 2026.
